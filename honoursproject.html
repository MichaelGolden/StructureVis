Honours Project (2011)


Project title

Identification of evolutionarily conserved genomic secondary structures within viral genomes.


Supervisor

Dr. Darren Martin


Abstract

The genomes of single stranded DNA and RNA viruses often contain numerous nucleic acid secondary structures. Whilst a number of these structures have been found to play important roles during the life cycles of these viruses, most have neither any identified function nor any known impact on viral fitness. Using an array of bioinformatic techniques we investigated the influence of inferred secondary structures on sequence evolution in the single stranded RNA virus, Hepatitis C Virus. We detected a strong association between structured regions of the genome and selective contraints on synonymous substitutions in coding regions, which suggests either that selection is acting to preserve these structures, or that the structures themselves lower mutation rates at these sites. In addition, coevolution analyses have revealed that there is a significant tendency for nucleotides which form part of structures to coevolve in a complementary fashion. Together these lines of evidence support the notion that structures which form in the genome are under strong evolutionary selection. Finally, we use data from the various analyses to rank structures within the Hepatitis C virus genome in order of their likely biologically relevance.
